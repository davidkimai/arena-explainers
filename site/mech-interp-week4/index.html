01 / synthesis

What the lesson is really teaching

The shared arc across all sources
  • Start with transformer recall. Embedding and unembedding already capture next-token tendencies, especially bigram-like statistics in simpler settings.
  • Introduce circuits. Attention can be factorized into QK and OV, and whole transformer behavior can be reasoned about as path composition.
  • Show a simple circuit first. Skip trigrams explain how one-layer attention can implement useful but limited pattern completion.
  • Then show the real star. Two-layer induction circuits explain copying and the emergence of stronger in-context learning.
  • Finally operationalize it. TransformerLens turns the theory into things you can inspect, cache, score, ablate, and attribute.
Why the transcript adds value beyond the slides

The transcript fills in the intuition that the slides compress. It repeatedly emphasizes:

  • the residual stream as output accumulation / communication channel,
  • the idea that heads work in smaller subspaces,
  • why one-layer heads cannot do conditional multi-step reasoning,
  • and why induction is exciting from an emergence and alignment perspective.
“The lesson is: don’t stop at visualizing patterns. Turn observations into detectors, then into causal tests.” Condensed from the transcript, ARENA notes, and slide exercises on detector functions, logit attribution, and ablation.
02 / mechanism

The core transformer circuit story

All three sources frame mechanistic interpretability as a shift from components to paths. The useful unit is not “the model” but the specific route information takes through embeddings, attention heads, residual stream, and unembedding.

    H -.-> G

Direct path

Embedding straight to unembedding explains why a degenerate or zero-layer picture mostly captures bigram statistics. This is the “predict from the current token identity” baseline.

QK circuit

Where should this token look? QK determines preferred source positions. In the notes and slides, this becomes the “which earlier token should I attend to?” part of the algorithm.

OV circuit

What consequence should following that source have? OV decides what gets written back, which is why direct logit attribution can often identify specific heads as major contributors.

Most important conceptual upgrade: the residual stream is not just “the hidden state.” In this lesson it is explicitly treated as a shared bus. Heads read from it, write back into it, and later heads can compose with earlier heads if their subspaces align enough.
03 / circuits

Skip trigrams vs induction heads

Mechanism Layer requirement What it does Why it matters Limitation
Skip trigram circuit Often 1 layer Uses QK + OV to complete patterns like “keep ... in mind” or other A ... B → C style local motifs. Shows that even simple attention can memorize and deploy structured local patterns. Because components in the same layer act in parallel, they cannot do the sequential conditional logic needed for true induction.
Previous-token head Layer 1 role Looks one token back and copies the previous token’s identity into a useful residual-stream subspace. Provides the setup needed for a later head to reason about “what follows this token?” By itself it only transports local information, it does not complete the multi-step copy pattern.
Induction head Needs 2+ layers Attends to the token after an earlier occurrence of the current token, enabling sequence copying like [A][B] ... [A] → [B]. This is the lesson’s first concrete example of in-context learning as a circuit, not a vague emergent mystery. Cannot exist in one layer because attention scores at one position cannot directly depend on the value of an adjacent earlier token unless another layer has already written that information forward.

What the slides stress

Induction heads are cool because they emerge, produce a visible loss change, and degrade performance when ablated.

What the transcript stresses

They matter because they are the first place where you can point to a concrete multi-step mechanism and say, “this is why the model can copy from context.”

What the notes stress

You can identify them operationally by running on repeated random sequences and scoring attention on the characteristic offset diagonal.

04 / tooling

TransformerLens as the operational interface

The notes and slide deck are unusually aligned here. TransformerLens is introduced not as a giant abstraction layer, but as a small set of high-leverage handles that make interpretability work feel experimentally tractable.

Core objects and methods
  • HookedTransformer.from_pretrained(...) or from_config(...)
  • model.to_tokens, to_str_tokens, to_string
  • model.run_with_cache(...) for activations
  • model.run_with_hooks(...) for interventions
  • shortcut weight access like model.W_Q, model.W_E, model.W_U
Why this matters pedagogically

The course repeatedly moves from “see the pattern” to “turn it into a detector” to “test it with ablation.” TransformerLens lowers the cost of every stage in that loop, which is why the notes frame it as making exploratory research feel fast and playful.

06 / action

Practical study guide, what to internalize from Week 4

If you only retain five things
  1. Embedding → unembedding already gives a baseline bigram-style predictor.
  2. Attention can be usefully split into QK for selection and OV for effect.
  3. One-layer attention can implement structured local motifs like skip trigrams, but not true induction.
  4. Induction heads are a two-layer circuit built from previous-token transport plus composition in a later head.
  5. TransformerLens is valuable because it lets you test claims causally, not just admire patterns.
Suggested working order
  1. Read slide sections on virtual weights, skip trigrams, and induction.
  2. Use the transcript to lock in the intuition for why two layers are required.
  3. Work the notebook exercises directly, especially detector functions and repeated-sequence tests.
  4. Only after that, move into hooks, ablation, and logit attribution.
Best one-sentence summary

Week 4 teaches you to stop treating in-context learning as magic and start treating it as a circuit you can localize, visualize, score, and break.
